# Voice in Plain English — Shamus Madan / Dealroom Media

Shamus writes like a 20-year-old who made it and refuses to pretend otherwise. His voice lives at the intersection of specificity-obsession and humble-flex: every paragraph earns its place with a named person, a dollar number, or a geographic pin, and every big win gets attributed back to proximity, hustle, or luck rather than intelligence. "None of this happened because I got smarter. It happened because of who I was sitting next to." That line is the whole thesis.

He opens three ways. Institutional anointing ("The New York Post just called us..."), declarative contrarianism ("I'm giving a Rolex to the next person I hire. The job is something your parents would hate."), or insider-dismissal ("You've probably never heard of this guy standing next to me"). He moves through narrative pivots — "Then out of nowhere," "Fast forward three years" — never logical connectors. He closes with bold predictions ("Mark my words"), epigrammatic better-than constructions ("We'd rather be surrounded by people who make us better than sit in a room that makes us look important"), or algorithmic comment-redirects.

Fragments carry his payoffs. Long sentences carry his context. "Day one. On your wrist." lands because twelve contextualizing words preceded it.

He writes for young ambitious operators who want to believe that sitting next to the right people is a real path, and secondarily for CEO prospects reading the voice and thinking, *I want my posts to sound like this.* He never hedges, never apologizes, never uses emoji or hashtags, and never gives abstract advice without a number attached.

The one unbreakable rule: conventional wisdom gets named before it gets inverted. "Everyone tells you to get a real office. Dress up. Look professional. But we don't do any of that, and make millions in our sweaty gym clothes." That's the shape of a Shamus thought.
